Capstone Report

High-Yield Topics for Sudan's Current Health Situation

Every module in this guide has flagged Sudan-specific relevance where it arose naturally in a question. This closing report pulls those threads together: what is actually happening in Sudan's health system right now, why it matters for the exam's community-medicine principles, and where the two intersect. Sources are WHO, UNICEF, OCHA and UN News, current as of early July 2026 ‚Äî cited inline, with the understanding that an active conflict situation continues to evolve.

Timeline note The cholera epidemic that dominated headlines through 2024‚Äì2025 was declared over on 3 March 2026 after a sustained response. A new (third) outbreak was declared on 29 June 2026 in West Kordofan ‚Äî just days before this report was written. Sudan's WHO country chief has described the shift from cholera's historic every-3-years cyclic pattern to now near-continuous outbreaks, a direct consequence of the ongoing war. Any individual Sudan-callout elsewhere in this guide describing "the current cholera epidemic" should be read against this fuller timeline.

1. The driver: three years of war, the world's largest humanitarian crisis

Conflict between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) erupted in April 2023 and has not stopped. By mid-2026, Sudan is described by WHO and UN agencies as the world's largest humanitarian crisis: an estimated 33.7 million people ‚Äî roughly two-thirds of the population ‚Äî need humanitarian assistance in 2026, 21 million need health services, and 13.6 million people are displaced (about 9.3 million internally, the rest across borders into Chad, South Sudan, Egypt and beyond) ‚Äî the largest displacement crisis in the world. WHO has verified 217 attacks on health care since the war began, causing over 2,000 deaths and hundreds of injuries among patients and health workers. 37% of health facilities are non-functional, and only 63% remain even partially operational.

Why this matters for the exam: nearly every downstream problem below ‚Äî outbreak frequency, EPI coverage, maternal mortality, malnutrition ‚Äî traces back to this single root cause. Questions asking "why does X persist in Sudan" almost always have "conflict-driven health-system collapse" as part of the answer.

2. Cholera: from a single epidemic to a recurring pattern

The 2024‚Äì2026 wave began in Kassala State in July 2024, spread to all 18 states, and by the time it was declared over on 3 March 2026 had infected 124,418 people and killed 3,573 ‚Äî a case-fatality ratio around 2.6‚Äì2.9%, above the 1% threshold that signals an inadequately controlled emergency. Control relied on a massive oral cholera vaccination effort (over 24 million people vaccinated), WASH restoration, and surveillance strengthening.

That success was short-lived: a new outbreak was declared on 29 June 2026 in West Kordofan ‚Äî the frontline dividing SAF- and RSF-held territory ‚Äî with 120 deaths and over 1,100 suspected cases already recorded, and early spread reported into neighbouring North Kordofan. This is Sudan's third cholera wave in three years. WHO's Sudan representative has explicitly noted that cholera, once cyclical roughly every three years, now recurs continuously because of the conflict, access constraints, and chronic WASH damage ‚Äî and the approaching rainy season (July‚ÄìOctober) is expected to accelerate transmission further.

Exam link: this is a live illustration of core epidemiology concepts tested throughout Module 1 ‚Äî attack rate, case-fatality ratio, epidemic curve shape (a "continuing common-source" pattern sustained by ongoing WASH failure rather than a single point-source), and the rationale for oral cholera vaccine as a reactive tool rather than a substitute for safe water and sanitation.

3. Malaria: Sudan's vaccine milestone, undercut by access

Sudan became the first country in the WHO Eastern Mediterranean Region to introduce a malaria vaccine into routine immunization, beginning in Gedaref and Blue Nile states in November 2024 (over 148,000 children under 12 months reached in the initial rollout) and expanding toward 129 localities through 2025‚Äì2026. Despite this, malaria remains one of the leading killers of children in Sudan: 2023 figures already showed over 1.3 million cases and 850+ deaths, with children bearing a disproportionate share of both, and current reporting ‚Äî now spanning 16 of Sudan's 18 states ‚Äî is widely believed to understate the true burden because of disrupted surveillance in conflict zones.

Exam link: ties directly to the Communicable Diseases module's malaria-vaccine and vector-control questions, and to Module 1's recurring "prevalence ‚âà incidence √ó duration" and surveillance-underreporting themes.

4. A crowded field of concurrent outbreaks

Cholera and malaria are not the only active outbreaks. WHO and UNICEF situation reports through 2026 describe simultaneous outbreaks of dengue (14 states), measles, circulating vaccine-derived poliovirus type 2 (cVDPV2), hepatitis E, meningitis, and diphtheria ‚Äî a pattern typical of a health system that has lost the surveillance and routine-immunization capacity to prevent multiple vaccine-preventable and vector-borne diseases from circulating at once. Measles catch-up campaigns (over 800,000 children reached in Darfur alone) and large-scale multi-antigen vaccination drives (46 million doses of cholera, polio, diphtheria, measles and rubella vaccine reported) are running in parallel just to hold the line.

Exam link: the co-occurrence of a live poliovirus outbreak (cVDPV2) is a direct, current illustration of why maintaining OPV coverage and cold-chain integrity ‚Äî tested repeatedly in the Communicable Diseases module ‚Äî is not a theoretical exercise. A poliovirus outbreak in 2026 shows what happens when routine EPI coverage collapses.

5. Malnutrition: the compounding crisis

Over 4 million people are estimated to be acutely malnourished in 2026. In North Darfur alone, nearly 85,000 children with severe acute malnutrition (SAM) were treated between January and November 2025 ‚Äî roughly one child every six minutes. WHO-supported stabilization centres have treated well over 100,000 children with complicated SAM nationally. Malnutrition and infectious disease reinforce each other: malnourished children tolerate diarrhoeal and vector-borne illness far worse, and outbreak-driven displacement worsens food access ‚Äî a textbook vicious cycle.

Exam link: directly relevant to the Non-Communicable Diseases module's nutrition questions (vitamin A/iodine deficiency, anthropometric screening, growth monitoring) and to child-health danger-sign content in Clinical/Other (IMCI, SAM management).

6. Maternal and child health under acute strain

An estimated 8.1 million women and girls of reproductive age ‚Äî including over 803,000 pregnant women ‚Äî require urgent reproductive health services, with roughly 1.1 million births expected in Sudan in 2026 alone. With over a third of health facilities non-functional, access to skilled birth attendance and emergency obstetric care (the two levers proven to reduce maternal mortality, as tested in the MCH module) has narrowed exactly when need is highest. Historically high female genital mutilation prevalence (~87% in women 15‚Äì49) continues to compound obstetric risk independent of the conflict.

Exam link: reinforces the MCH module's core teaching that maternal mortality reduction depends on skilled birth attendance and comprehensive emergency obstetric care ‚Äî precisely the services conflict has degraded.

7. An under-addressed layer: mental health

UN and WHO reporting increasingly flags widespread psychological distress across Sudan's population ‚Äî depression, anxiety, and post-traumatic stress disorder linked to prolonged exposure to violence, displacement, and loss ‚Äî alongside major, largely unmet gaps in mental health service capacity. This connects directly to the Clinical/Other module's psychiatry content (depression, PTSD-adjacent anxiety presentations) and is a growing, if less visible, component of Sudan's overall disease burden.
